# LinkedIn Style Patterns

The structural DNA of LinkedIn posts that stop the scroll and get shared — captured as patterns, not copy. Use this in Steps 3 and 5 as the stylistic north star *after* you've loaded the user's actual voice from CLAUDE.md or their own post examples.

**Important:** these patterns describe the *shape* of strong LinkedIn content. The *voice* — specific phrases, tone, word choice — comes from the user. Don't pull voice from this document.

---

## 1. Sentence length and rhythm

The #1 thing that makes a post sound human vs AI.

- **Average sentence: 7-12 words.** If you're writing 20+ word sentences, break them up.
- **Every new thought gets its own line.** No exceptions.
- **Rhythm:** short statement → line break → expansion → line break → contrast → line break → insight.
- **Fragments are good.** "In real time." "That was still me." "Node by node." Real writing uses them.
- Test: read the draft back. If any line contains two thoughts, split it.

---

## 2. Line breaks, not paragraphs

LinkedIn is not a blog. Dense paragraphs tank scroll depth.

- Paragraphs: 1-2 sentences max, then a line break.
- Every thought lives on its own line.
- Visual whitespace does the structural work headings would do in a blog.
- Never stack more than 3 short lines in a row without a visual break (arrow list, bold line, CTA).

---

## 3. Arrow bullets, not dashes

LinkedIn's native feel uses arrows, not bullet points. Three options:

- `↳` (return arrow)
- `➝` (right arrow)
- `→` (simple right arrow)

Pick one per post and stay consistent. Never use `-` or `•` — they read as corporate.

Use bullets for:
- Listed failures / pain points
- Step enumerations
- Category breakdowns (Health / Social / Work)
- Quick outcome lists

Don't use bullets when prose with line breaks would read better.

---

## 4. Bold Unicode for section headers (sparingly)

Inside a longer post, Unicode bold (like 𝗛𝗼𝘄 𝘁𝗼 𝘅) can mark section pivots. Convert via any Unicode bold converter.

**Rules:**
- Max 2-3 bold Unicode lines per post
- Use for tutorial step titles ("𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟭:", "𝗦𝘁𝗲𝗽 𝟮:")
- Never use for body copy — it's for structural beats only
- Insight posts and story posts often work better with zero bold — the rhythm carries it

---

## 5. Opening patterns (the hook)

Three patterns that consistently earn the "see more" click:

**Bold statement / counterintuitive insight**
> "Most [role] don't realize they're sitting on a goldmine."
> "The best [role] I know all do [unexpected thing]."

**Personal confession / vulnerability**
> "2 years ago, I changed the way I set goals. It changed my life."
> "My first startup failed because I spent too much time [thing]."

**Direct challenge to a common belief**
> "If you want to [goal] in [timeframe]: don't spend more than 30 days [thing]."
> "Stop [common practice]. Start [counter-practice]."

See `../templates/hook-templates.md` for 80+ specific templates with psychological triggers. This file covers the *opening patterns*; the templates file covers the *exact sentence shapes*.

---

## 6. Hook-to-body flow

This is where posts most commonly fail. The hook and the next line must feel like one continuous thought.

The hook delivers the "what." The very next line should be the reader's natural next thought. Ask: "If someone just read this hook out loud, what would I naturally say next?"

**Good flow example (pattern — plug in the user's voice):**
> HOOK: "Most [role] don't realize they're sitting on a goldmine."
> NEXT LINE: "They think [thing the role thinks] is only for [who they think it's for]."

The second line is the logical extension — not a restatement.

**Common failures to avoid:**
- **Restating the hook** in different words (reader already read it — move forward)
- **Abrupt topic jump** ("Great hook about X... anyway, here's Y")
- **Backstory before payoff** when the hook already set up the frame

---

## 7. Tone posture

- **Direct and confident**, never hedging or wishy-washy
- **Conversational** — write like telling a smart friend what happened, not structuring an argument
- **"I" and "you" freely** — it's personal
- **Vulnerability where natural** — real experience, not performance
- **Zero buzzword soup** — no "game-changer," "unlock," "harness," "leverage," "landscape"
- **Contrarian when warranted** — challenge conventional thinking when the user genuinely disagrees with it
- **Inspirational but grounded** — no hype without substance

---

## 8. Flow — each line must be the logical next thought

Read each line and ask: "Does this naturally follow from the line above?"

- The post should feel like a person talking, not a structured document with sections.
- Transitions should be invisible. If you need a header or a "Now let's talk about..." to change topics, you're covering too many ideas.
- Standalone transition words work when they flow naturally:
  - "Why?" (setting up a single-line answer)
  - "The result?" (teeing up an outcome)
  - "Here's what happened:" (entering a story beat)

Use these sparingly — one or two per post is plenty.

---

## 9. Content approach

- **Lead with insight, not information.** "Most [role] are [counterintuitive observation]" beats "Here's something interesting about [topic]."
- **Every paragraph must teach, challenge, or inspire.** If it doesn't, cut it.
- **Specific examples over generic advice.** Numbers, names, timeframes.
- **Frameworks must be immediately actionable.** No abstract wisdom without a next step.
- **Personal experience where relevant.** Use the user's real stories from context — don't fabricate.
- **End strong.** The last 2-3 lines carry disproportionate weight. No "thanks for reading" fade.

---

## 10. Length

- **150-300 words.** LinkedIn sweet spot.
- Under 150 → usually too thin to drive engagement.
- Over 300 → dropoff accelerates fast.
- The post should feel complete but not exhausted. If the user needs more space, it's a two-part post or a newsletter.

---

## 11. Engagement mechanics

**Soft CTA or question at the end** to drive comments.
- Question-based: "What are yours for [year]?"
- Open invitation: "Curious how others are solving this."
- Challenge: "Try it this week. Report back."

**Optional comment-based engagement hook** mid-post or end:
- "Comment '[keyword]' and I'll send you [resource]."
- Only use when the resource is real and the CTA is in the user's business context. Never fabricate a resource.

**Hashtags — not in the post body.** If used at all, 3-5 at the very bottom separated by a line break. Most strong posts skip them entirely.

**Emojis — sparingly.** 1-2 max, only if they add something. Usually at the end of a CTA or question line, not mid-paragraph.

---

## 12. Post types that work

From most to least common, roughly in order of what ships well:

1. **Insight / Mindset shift** — a belief the user holds that the audience probably doesn't. Most common high-performer.
2. **Tutorial / How-to** — step-by-step with copy-paste or screen-worthy outputs. Uses bold Unicode section headers and arrow lists.
3. **Personal / Life advice** — a life rule, framework, or system the user uses. Often leads with "X years ago..." and lands on a transferable principle.
4. **Success share** — specific result + how it was earned + what the reader can do. Works only with real numbers.
5. **Contrarian take** — challenges a widespread assumption in the user's field. Requires genuine disagreement, not invented edge.

Each type has different hook templates — see `../templates/hook-templates.md` for which templates match which post type.

---

## 13. What NOT to do

- Don't write walls of text. If a paragraph is more than 2 sentences, break it up.
- Don't open with "I'm excited to share..." or any LinkedIn cliché.
- Don't use regular bullet points (`•` or `-`) — use arrows.
- Don't hashtag inside the post body.
- Don't cram multiple ideas into explicit sections — one idea with depth, secondaries as undertones.
- Don't bolt the body onto the hook — they must flow as one continuous thought.
- Don't sound like AI — no "In today's fast-paced world," no "Let's dive in," no "Here's the thing you need to understand," no "game-changer," no "landscape."
- Don't over-explain — trust the reader's intelligence.
- Don't end with "thanks for reading" or "hope this helps" — soft fades kill engagement.

---

## How to combine these patterns with the user's voice

These patterns describe the **shape** of a strong LinkedIn post. The user's voice context (from CLAUDE.md or their own posts) describes the **sound**.

Use this order:

1. **Pick the shape** (length, hook pattern, arrow usage, section structure) from this file.
2. **Fill the shape with the user's voice** — their word choice, signature phrases, tone, typical cadence.
3. If there's a conflict between a pattern here and the user's voice — **voice wins.** These patterns are defaults, not commandments.

The goal: a post that has the structural bones of a high-performing LinkedIn post *and* sounds like the user genuinely wrote it.
