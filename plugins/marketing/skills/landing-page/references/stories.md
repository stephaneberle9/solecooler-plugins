# Stories — Introduction, Problem, and Solution

The story section is Step 2 of writing (but Section 3 on the page). Start here — it's the easiest section and sets the voice for the rest.

Load this when it's time to draft the story.

---

## Story structure — 3 parts

### Part A — Introduce yourself

Name, relevant credentials, why they should listen. Short. Just enough for the next part to land.

Three patterns:

**Short version (minimal credentials):**
```
My name's [Name].

I'm a [role/profession] who's been [doing the relevant thing] for [timeframe].
```

**With credentials:**
```
My name's [Name].

I've been a [specific role] for [X years]. My work's [specific measurable result]. And I've worked across [range of contexts/markets].
```

**Relatable version (no credentials yet):**
```
I'm [Name]. I'm not a [expert type]. I don't [typical expert skill]. Honestly, I couldn't even [humble admission].

But I figured out how to [specific thing related to offer]. And now I [do it regularly/successfully].
```

### Part B — The problem (tried and failed)

Paint the pain vividly. Walk through three things you tried that didn't work. Each failure has a specific reason. Then the trigger-point insight.

Structure:

```
1. Describe the problem vividly (1-3 sentences, specifics and sensory detail)
2. "So I tried [solution 1]... but [why it failed]"
3. "Then I tried [solution 2]... but [why it failed]"
4. "I even tried [solution 3]... but [why it failed]"
5. "That's when I realized [trigger-point insight]"
```

**Why this works:** Each failure disqualifies a competing solution the reader has probably already tried. Skipping the failures makes the story sound like a guru pitch.

### Part C — Finding the solution

How the problem got solved. Not the tactical how — the origin how.

Structure:

```
1. "So I started [research / experimentation / observation]"
2. "I discovered / built / figured out [the solution]"
3. "I tested it and [specific result]"
4. "Then other people started asking about it"
5. "So I decided to turn it into [the product]"
```

This section ends with a line that transitions directly into the Offer section. Something like: "And that's what became [Product Name]."

---

## Rules

- **Use specifics.** "200 cold emails" beats "a lot of emails." "$800" beats "almost nothing." "Thursday at 2am" beats "one night."
- **Include the failures.** Three of them. Skip them and it sounds like a guru pitch.
- **Keep it moving.** 300-500 words total. Don't get lost in backstory.
- **End on the transition line.** The offer section has to pick up the momentum.

## Introduction examples

**Short (for someone early-stage):**
```
My name's Sam.

I'm a marketer who's spent the last two years figuring out how to make AI tools actually work for small business content, not just flashy demos.
```

**With credentials (for someone established):**
```
My name's Jordan Chen.

I've been a direct-response copywriter for 14 years. My copy has made over $80M for clients across software, coaching, and home services. And I've worked in markets ranging from roofing to SaaS to fitness coaching.
```

**Relatable (for a non-expert angle):**
```
I'm Alex. I'm not a developer. I don't know how to code. Honestly, I needed help setting up my own email forwarding two years ago.

But I figured out how to use modern AI tools to run 80% of my agency's marketing ops. And now I do it for 30 minutes a day.
```

## Problem examples (tried and failed)

### Problem example — learning a new tool/skill

```
Last year I kept seeing people talk about [tool/method] on Twitter. Everyone seemed to be using it. Building things. Automating workflows. Creating output faster than anyone I knew.

So I tried to learn it.

I watched YouTube tutorials... but they were either too technical or too basic. I'd follow along, get stuck, and have no idea why.

I read the documentation... but it assumed you already knew things I didn't know. Every answer led to three more questions.

I even paid for a course... but it was made for developers. Within 20 minutes I was lost.

I felt like everyone else got it except me. Like there was some secret handshake I was missing.

Then I realized: there was no guide for people like me. Non-technical people who just wanted to use this thing for their actual work.
```

### Problem example — getting clients (freelance / service)

```
When I started freelancing, I had no idea how to get clients.

I tried cold emailing... but nobody replied. I sent 200 emails that first month. Got maybe 3 responses. Zero clients.

I tried posting on social media... but I had 47 followers. My posts got 2 likes. Both from my mom.

I even tried freelance platforms... but I was competing with people who charged $5/hour. I couldn't win on price and didn't know how to win on anything else.

After 3 months, I'd made $800. I was burning through my savings. I was about to give up and get a "real job."

That's when I stumbled onto something that changed everything.
```

### Problem example — pricing (freelance / consulting)

```
For years I charged $50/hour. I told myself that was "good money."

Then a client mentioned what they paid their previous freelancer. It was 4x my rate. For the same work.

I felt sick. How much money had I left on the table?

So I tried raising my rates... but I chickened out at the last second. I was terrified of losing clients.

I tried adding "premium packages"... but clients just picked the cheap option and asked for the premium deliverables anyway.

I even tried a price increase email... but I spent 3 weeks writing it, never sent it, and went back to charging $50/hour.

I was trapped. I knew I was undercharging, but I didn't know how to charge more without losing everything I'd built.
```

### Problem example — SaaS trial conversion

```
A year in, our SaaS had 400 trial signups a month and about 18 paying customers from it. Four and a half percent. Dismal.

So I tried rewriting the onboarding emails. Sent six new variants over eight weeks. Moved the needle by a point.

I tried redesigning the first-run experience. Worked with a designer, shipped a new flow. Conversion actually went down.

I even ran weekly user interviews. Talked to 40 trial users in six weeks. They all said the product was "great" and then never paid.

I was out of ideas and burning runway. Then I read something that made me rethink the whole thing.

What I'd been optimizing — the sign-up flow, the emails, the UI — wasn't the bottleneck. The bottleneck was what happened in the first 15 minutes. And I'd been ignoring it because it felt "too simple to matter."
```

### Problem example — teaching / course creation

```
I'd been selling 1:1 coaching for three years. Solid business. Burned out.

I tried building a course... but I couldn't figure out what to include. Every draft outline had 47 modules.

I tried hiring a course strategist... but they wanted $15k to tell me things I already knew.

I even tried just recording 10 hours of video and calling it a course... but looking at the edit queue killed the project.

18 months later, zero course launched. Still 1:1 coaching. Still burned out.

Then I watched a friend launch her first course to $80k in five days using a method that skipped 90% of what I'd been told was "required."
```

## Solution examples (finding / building it)

### Solution example — learning a new tool/skill

```
So I decided to figure it out myself. The hard way.

I spent 3 months trying everything. Breaking things. Getting frustrated. Slowly piecing together what actually worked.

I documented every step. Every mistake. Every "aha" moment. I wrote down exactly what I wished someone had told me on day one.

Eventually, it clicked. I wasn't just using the tool — I was using it for hours every day. Writing content. Building workflows. Doing things that used to take me all week in a single afternoon.

Friends started asking how I did it. "Can you just show me your setup?" Colleagues wanted to know what workflows I was using.

So I put together a simple walkthrough. Just the basics. Just enough to get someone from zero to actually using the thing.

And that's what became [Product Name].
```

### Solution example — getting clients

```
So I started paying attention to freelancers who were fully booked.

Not the ones with huge audiences. The ones with small followings who still had more work than they could handle. What were they doing differently?

I noticed a pattern. They weren't doing more. They were doing something specific that everyone else ignored.

I tested it. Within 3 weeks I had more leads than I'd had in the previous 3 months combined.

I refined the system. Documented it. Turned it into a repeatable process.

By month 6 I was fully booked. By month 12 I had a waitlist.

Other freelancers started asking what I was doing. I showed a few of them. They got results too.

That's when I realized this wasn't just something that worked for me. This was a system that worked — across niches, experience levels, and price points.

And that's what became [Product Name].
```

### Solution example — SaaS trial conversion

```
So I went back to the basics. Ignored the funnel diagrams. Ignored the "best practices" blog posts.

I just sat with 12 trial users and watched them open the product for the first time. Recorded every session.

A pattern showed up in minute three. The moment users hit a specific screen, they either "got it" and kept going, or they closed the tab and never came back.

I stripped that screen down to a single CTA. Rebuilt the first-run around three specific jobs, not thirty features. Added one email that landed five minutes after signup with the single thing users needed to unblock.

Trial-to-paid went from 4.5% to 14%. Inside 60 days. No other changes.

We tried it on two more SaaS companies. Same result. Different numbers, but meaningful double-digit lifts every time.

Other founders started asking me to run the same audit on their onboarding. After ten engagements, I packaged the whole thing up.

And that's what became [Product Name].
```

### Solution example — teaching / course creation

```
So I watched what my friend actually did. Step by step. Didn't assume I knew.

She wasn't teaching "everything she knew." She was teaching one specific transformation. Start state → end state. In 4 weeks. That was it.

No 47-module monster. No 10-hour video library. A focused program that did one thing and did it well.

I copied the framework on my first course. Cut my draft outline from 47 modules to 6. Launched in 3 weeks.

First launch: $23k. Second launch, 6 weeks later: $58k. Third launch, 90 days in: $112k with a waitlist.

Friends started asking what I was doing. I broke down the exact framework. Two of them launched their first courses to $30k+.

That's when I realized this wasn't a me-thing. It was a repeatable system for turning coaching knowledge into a focused first course.

And that's what became [Product Name].
```

## Story gotchas

1. **Generic failures.** "I tried lots of things but nothing worked" is not a story. Three specific things, each with a specific reason.

2. **Skipping the failures entirely.** Removes credibility. Makes the user sound like someone who got lucky and is now selling the luck.

3. **Credentials section that runs long.** More than 3-4 lines of "my name is X and I've done Y" stalls the story before it starts.

4. **No transition line.** The story has to hand off to the offer section. "And that's what became [Product Name]" is a working template.

5. **Fabricated specifics.** "200 emails" is better than "a lot" — but only if the user actually sent 200 emails. When numbers are missing from the business context, use `[PLACEHOLDER: specific number]` so the user fills it in.

6. **Teaching in the story.** The story doesn't teach. It earns credibility. Save the "how it works" for the offer section.
