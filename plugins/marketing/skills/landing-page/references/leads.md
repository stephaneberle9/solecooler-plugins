# Leads — The 8-Step Formula

The lead is the 150-200 words right after the headline. Think "teaser trailer," not "summary." Its only job is to earn the next scroll.

Load this when it's time to write the lead (Step 5).

---

## The 8-step lead formula

Each step is one to two sentences. Skip any step only if the headline already covered it.

### Step 1 — Call them out

Name exactly who this is for. Specific. In their words. Pulled from ICP context.

Examples:
- "If you're a service professional struggling to find clients..."
- "If you've been posting content for months with nothing to show for it..."
- "If you keep hearing about AI but can't figure out how to actually use it..."
- "If you're a freelancer tired of the feast-or-famine cycle..."
- "If you run a SaaS and your trial conversion is stuck under 5%..."
- "If you're a coach who can't break past $10k months no matter what you try..."
- "If you've built a list but your open rates keep sliding..."

### Step 2 — Make them a promise

The big outcome. Bold but believable.

Examples:
- "...then I'm about to share how you can get an endless stream of qualified leads..."
- "...I'm going to show you exactly how to build an audience that actually buys..."
- "...you're about to discover the simplest way to use AI for your marketing..."
- "...I'm going to hand you the system that took our pilot group from $3k months to $15k months..."
- "...I'll walk you through the exact 3-pillar onboarding flow that lifted our clients' trial-to-paid by 38% on average..."

### Step 3 — Within a timeframe

Specific. "Soon" kills credibility.

Examples:
- "...in the next 7 days (or even sooner)."
- "...within 30 days of implementing what I'm about to show you."
- "...by this time next week."
- "...in a single afternoon."
- "...before your next launch."
- "...inside 90 days."

### Step 4 — How it works (brief mechanism)

Hint at the unique method. Do not explain it. Tease.

Examples:
- "Using a brand new, little-known traffic source that's completely free and works for any service business."
- "Using a simple 3-part content system that takes 30 minutes a day."
- "Using the exact same setup and workflows that 500+ non-technical users have already adopted."
- "Using a counterintuitive pricing psychology that makes clients actually want to pay more."
- "Using the '3-pillar activation' flow we refined across 200+ SaaS clients."

### Step 5 — Handle objections

Address the biggest doubt before the reader thinks it.

Examples:
- "And this will get you clients even if you're brand new and don't have any testimonials."
- "This works whether you have 100 followers or 100,000."
- "You don't need any technical skills. If you can follow simple instructions, you can do this."
- "This works even if you've never raised your rates before. Even if you're terrified of losing clients."
- "It works for B2B or B2C. Courses, coaching, or services."

### Step 6 — Easy and/or quick

Remove friction. Make it feel achievable.

Examples:
- "It's almost entirely automated and takes less than an hour a day."
- "The whole setup takes 20 minutes. I'll walk you through every click."
- "No complicated tech. No confusing dashboards. Just follow the steps."
- "About a week to implement. And once it's running, you'll wonder how you worked without it."

### Step 7 — Add proof

Numbers, results, social proof. Pulled from the business context. Never fabricated.

Examples:
- "Plus, it's already helped 370+ professionals get fully booked."
- "This is the same system behind $2.3M in sales for our pilot group."
- "We've taught this to 3,000+ students and watched it work across dozens of industries."
- "200+ freelancers have used this to add $50k+ to their annual income."
- "In our pilot cohort, 18 out of 22 launched their first program to $25k+."

If the user doesn't have proof yet, skip Step 7 and lean harder on Step 5 (objection handling) and Step 8 (transition into the story).

### Step 8 — Transition out

Bridge into the story. Natural handoff.

Examples:
- "Though first, let me introduce myself..."
- "But before I explain how it all works, I need to tell you a quick story..."
- "Let me explain how I stumbled onto this..."
- "Here's how I figured this out the hard way..."
- "But first, the short version of how this came together..."

## Lead gotchas

1. **Over-explaining.** The lead is a teaser. If it's over 200 words, cut it. Details go in the offer section.

2. **Starting with "In today's world..."** Throat-clearing kills the first impression. Start with the "If you..." call-out.

3. **Promise with no timeframe.** Vague promises sound like every other sales page. Nail down Step 3.

4. **Fabricating Step 7 proof.** If the user doesn't have numbers, skip Step 7. Don't invent "2,000 students" to pad the proof.

5. **Transition that doesn't transition.** Step 8 must bridge into the story section. "Anyway, let's get to it" is not a bridge.
