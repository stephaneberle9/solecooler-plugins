# Newsletter Deep Techniques — Fundamentals & Advanced Moves

Reference material that supports the 8-step framework in SKILL.md. Read this when a step calls for it, or when a draft is technically correct but emotionally flat.

---

## the 5 fundamentals

Apply to every newsletter. These are the quality baseline — every section in every step should pass all five.

1. **Curious** — Say something useful in an interesting way. If the reader can predict the next sentence, you've already lost them.
2. **Clear** — Easy to understand, remember, and act on. One core idea per paragraph. No nested concepts.
3. **Conversational** — Write like you speak. Contractions, fragments, asides. If you wouldn't say it to a friend over coffee, don't write it.
4. **Concise** — Fewer words = more impact. Every word earns its place. Cut anything that doesn't change the meaning if removed.
5. **Credible** — Show authority without arrogance. Let the specifics (numbers, names, details) do the bragging. Never claim "I'm an expert."

## advanced techniques

These are the moves that turn a competent newsletter into one people screenshot and forward.

### Polarization

If no one hates it, no one loves it. The secret is contrast.

**Core Values Coin Flip:** What you stand for → What pisses you off about the opposite.

- Stand for: shipping fast → Hate: endless planning meetings
- Stand for: working alone → Hate: 9-person Slack channels for one decision
- Stand for: writing for one reader → Hate: "growth hack" engagement bait

Challenge conventions — what does your reader think:
- Is right that's actually wrong?
- Is wrong that's actually right?
- Is important but isn't?
- Is complicated that's actually simple?

Ask yourself: **What does 10% more courage look like?** Then write that version.

### HEAL Framework for Microstories

Use for any story embedded in the newsletter. Works for the opening anecdote, the proof story, or a quick aside in the body.

- **H**ere's what happened — grab the reader by the throat. Skip setup. Drop into the moment.
- **E**motion — what did you feel? Name it specifically. "Embarrassed" not "off." "Furious" not "annoyed."
- **A**ction — what did you do? Concrete moves, not vague reactions. "Closed the laptop and walked the dog for an hour" not "I needed to think."
- **L**esson — the takeaway. One sentence. The reader's takeaway, not yours.

### Section Structure (micro-architecture)

Each section inside the newsletter is a micro-piece with its own three-part structure:

1. **Hook** — grab attention for THIS section. A short line, a question, a stat, a one-line story beat.
2. **Body** — deliver the value. The proof, the detail, the unfolding.
3. **Payoff** — land the point with impact. The reader should feel "oh" or "huh" before moving to the next section.

**Test:** Every section should stand alone if extracted. If you copy-paste one section into a tweet draft, it should still hit. If it doesn't, the section isn't earning its place.

### Bouncing Between "Me" and "You"

Don't stay in your own head for more than 2 paragraphs without pulling the reader in. The pattern:

- 1-2 paragraphs: my story, my numbers, my mistake
- 1 paragraph: pivot to the reader — "You've probably done the same thing." / "Here's where this matters for you."
- 1-2 paragraphs: back to the proof or next story beat

This keeps the newsletter from becoming a diary entry. The reader needs to feel like the email is about THEM, even when you're telling YOUR story.

### The Slippery Slide

Every sentence's job is to make the reader read the next one. Tactics:

- Open paragraphs with short, punchy sentences
- End paragraphs with curiosity bombs ("That wasn't even the worst part.")
- Use micro-loops between sections ("Which brings me to the real lesson...")
- Vary sentence length aggressively. Short. Then medium. Then a longer one that breathes a bit before snapping shut.

### Sticky Statements

A sticky statement is a single line that could survive on its own as a tweet, a quote graphic, or a pull quote. Every newsletter should have at least one.

How to make one:
- Compress the entire newsletter's lesson into 8-15 words
- Use parallel structure ("Most X. Some Y. Almost no one Z.")
- Use specificity instead of abstraction
- End on a stressed syllable when possible (more memorable)

Examples:
- "Most creators are building their business backwards."
- "The 47 emails almost made me run. Then they made me stay."
- "Emails excite. Courses educate. Don't confuse the two."

### The 1/3rd Rule (the deeper version)

Cutting a third of the words isn't about brevity for its own sake. It's about removing the seams. First drafts are full of:

- Throat-clearing ("So basically what I want to say is...")
- Restating the previous sentence in new words
- Hedges ("kind of", "sort of", "in a way")
- Connective tissue that doesn't pull the reader anywhere ("And so", "Anyway", "Now")
- Adverbs that weaken verbs ("really", "actually", "very", "just")

Cut all of those. Then check word count. You'll usually be at ~67% of the original. That's the target.
