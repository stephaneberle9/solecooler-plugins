# Hook Patterns for Long-Form Tweets

The first line of your tweet decides everything. You get 0.5 seconds. If the opener doesn't stop the scroll, the rest doesn't matter.

These 8 hook patterns come from analysis of consistently viral content. Each includes the formula, real examples, and guidance on when to use it.

**How to use this reference:** Pick a hook pattern, write 2-3 variations, then pick the one that would stop YOU mid-scroll.

---

## Pattern 1: Shocking Statistic Lead

Open with a counterintuitive or alarming number that challenges assumptions.

**Formula:** [Shocking %/number] + [surprising outcome/context]

**Examples:**
- "~40% of cancer deaths could be prevented if caught at Stage 1."
- "Close to 50% of Americans will get cancer in their lifetime."
- "$10,000 per person on healthcare — that's what the US spends. Far more than Europe, yet Americans are sicker and die younger."
- "35% of your body's protein content is collagen. Your diet should reflect that."
- "They lost 861,000 people in 2023. By 2070, 1 in 3 of her citizens will have vanished."

**Best for:** Health, business, social trends, demographic shifts, market opportunities

**Pro tip:** Follow the stat immediately with "So" or "But" to drive the narrative forward. The stat creates the gap. The next sentence sets the direction.

**Adaptation for long-form tweets:** Use the stat as the first line, then build the post below it using Pattern 4 (Cultural Analysis) or Pattern 5 (Paradox Reveal) from the main skill.

## Pattern 2: Superlative + "Most Overlooked" Formula

Combine an extreme claim with underdog positioning.

**Formula:** [THING] is the most [overlooked/underrated/dangerous] [category] on Earth.

**Examples:**
- "NATTOKINASE is the most overlooked supplement on Earth."
- "BAKING SODA is the most overlooked medicine on Earth."
- "The most dangerous, common, and overlooked problem in the world: Stress."
- "The most sustainable way to lose weight: It's not biking. It's not running. It's not swimming. It's incline walking."

**Best for:** Unconventional solutions, contrarian takes, product recommendations, tools

**Pro tip:** ALL CAPS the subject for visual punch. Follow with specific benefits, not vague promises.

**Adaptation for long-form tweets:** Works well as the opener for Pattern 1 (Numbered Opportunity List) or Pattern 7 (Build-in-Public) from the main skill. Name the overlooked thing, then list why.

---

## Pattern 3: Authority Figure Introduction

Lead with a credible person and their dramatic claim or result.

**Formula:** This is [NAME]. [Their shocking achievement/position]. [Promise of revelation]

**Examples:**
- "This is Gary Brecka. He's the man who saved the life of UFC president Dana White. Gary applied a biohacking protocol on Dana that tripled his life expectancy."
- "This is Mark Hyman. The world's leading longevity expert. His message? Food is medicine."
- "This man changed the game: Mark Sisson. This 70-year-old defies aging by combining evolutionary biology and modern biohacking methods."
- "This is Tim Grover. The man behind Michael Jordan's legacy. He taught Jordan a concept that made him the greatest of all time..."

**Best for:** Profile-style posts, expert takes, controversial figures, case studies

**Pro tip:** Use "This is [NAME]" not "Meet [NAME]" for stronger impact. The period after their name creates a beat.

**Adaptation for long-form tweets:** Natural lead-in to Pattern 4 (Cultural Analysis) or Pattern 2 (Step-by-Step Framework). Introduce the person, then analyze their approach.

## Pattern 4: Contrarian / "X is a Scam" Opening

Challenge conventional wisdom with a provocative claim.

**Formula:** [Commonly accepted thing] is a scam / is dying / is wrong

**Examples:**
- "The death of drinking culture is here. For 1000s of years, alcohol has been at the center of social life. But today's younger generations are creating an entirely new social culture."
- "Japan is dying. They lost 861,000 people in 2023."
- "Almonds are a scam. They're loaded with anti-nutrients, enzyme inhibitors & deleterious pesticides."
- "Shampoo is a scam."
- "Big Pharma is fooling you again with Ozempic."
- "Your toothpaste might be poisoning you"

**Best for:** Product critiques, cultural shifts, debunking myths, industry hot takes

**Pro tip:** Must deliver substance. Bold claims without evidence in the body will get ratioed. Back it up.

**Adaptation for long-form tweets:** Perfect opener for Pattern 5 (Paradox Reveal) or Pattern 4 (Cultural Analysis). State the contrarian claim, then build the case below.

## Pattern 5: Dramatic Business Narrative

Tell a high-stakes story with an unexpected outcome.

**Formula:** [Company/Person] did [shocking action]. Everyone [reaction]. But [surprise twist].

**Examples:**
- "This Indian billionaire gave away $25 billion in mobile services for FREE. Everyone laughed... But 6 months later, he'd CRUSHED 3 of his biggest competitors. Now, he controls 40% of the market."
- "They gave him 3x more royalties than Michael Jordan. Their stock rose 150%. Until it cost them $1.3 billion in a single day."
- "The $171,000 lie that could have killed Tesla: Top Gear faked a negative review of the Tesla Roadster. Most companies would have stayed quiet. Tesla chose war."
- "Burberry burned $36,500,000 of clothes in one year. Not because they couldn't sell them. Because they didn't want YOU to have them."
- "Elon Musk fired 80% of Twitter and everyone thought Twitter was doomed. He was right. Everyone was wrong."

**Best for:** Business case studies, underdog stories, contrarian strategies, founder stories

**Pro tip:** "Everyone thought X. They were wrong." is one of the highest-performing formats for a reason. The reader immediately needs to know why.

**Adaptation for long-form tweets:** Natural opener for Pattern 3 (Bookend Reframe) or Pattern 4 (Cultural Analysis). Tell the dramatic setup, then unpack what actually happened.

## Pattern 6: Mystery Question Hook

Open with a paradox that demands explanation.

**Formula:** If [logical expectation], why [contradictory reality]?

**Examples:**
- "If pasta, bread, and red meat are so bad, why do Europeans live to 100+ and stay healthy? But when Americans consume these? They get chronically sick."
- "This doesn't make sense. Despite being low-income, Nicoya, Costa Rica has one of the highest life expectancies in the entire world. So how are they doing it?"
- "Notice how logos recently all look the same? Not because it makes them look better... But because of THIS psychological trick that manipulates your brain."

**Best for:** Comparative analysis, cultural differences, paradoxes, "why does X work but Y doesn't" posts

**Pro tip:** Frame as genuine curiosity, not a rhetorical question. The reader should feel the same confusion you're expressing.

**Adaptation for long-form tweets:** Sets up Pattern 5 (Paradox Reveal) perfectly. Ask the question, then spend the post answering it.

---

## Pattern 7: Numbered List Promise

Lead with a specific number of insights from authority or experience.

**Formula:** [#] [lessons/secrets/reasons] from [credible source]

**Examples:**
- "11 things I learned from one of the greatest philosophers of all time"
- "17 lessons from the most controversial book on entrepreneurship ever written"
- "Here are 7 health secrets Nicoyans know that Americans don't"
- "6 steps to improve your jawline & facial structure"
- "15 simple lifestyle changes to reset your nervous system"

**Best for:** Educational content, actionable advice, curated insights, "here's what I learned" posts

**Pro tip:** Odd numbers (7, 11, 13, 17) outperform round numbers (5, 10, 15). Higher numbers (11+) signal thoroughness. Lower numbers (5-7) signal focus.

**Adaptation for long-form tweets:** Direct opener for Pattern 1 (Numbered Opportunity List) or Pattern 2 (Step-by-Step Framework). The hook IS the promise. The body delivers.

---

## Pattern 8: "Everyone Thinks X, But Y" Reversal

Set up and demolish conventional wisdom.

**Formula:** Everyone thinks [common belief]. They are all wrong. [Contrarian truth]

**Examples:**
- "Everyone thinks the best cities for young entrepreneurs are: Miami. Dubai. London. They are all wrong. One city surpasses them all."
- "Everyone worships Andrew Huberman. But Stanford's most brilliant professor isn't a neuroscientist. He's worth $15 BILLION and still drives a 2012 minivan."
- "For a long time, dementia was seen as an inevitable part of aging. But according to new research: More than 40% of dementia cases are easily preventable."

**Best for:** Contrarian advice, hidden gems, overlooked solutions, "actually, the answer is..." posts

**Pro tip:** The reversal must be specific and unexpected. "Everyone thinks X is good but actually Y is better" is weak. "Everyone thinks X but they're looking at the wrong metric entirely" is strong.

**Adaptation for long-form tweets:** Opens Pattern 5 (Paradox Reveal) or Pattern 4 (Cultural Analysis). State the conventional wisdom, then dismantle it.

## Hook Stacking (Advanced)

Combine two patterns for stronger openers:

**Shocking Stat + Authority:**
"~40% of cancer deaths could be prevented at Stage 1. Peter Attia and Silicon Valley's elite just built the solution:"

**Contrarian + Mystery:**
"Shampoo is a scam. Here's how you can improve hair thickness without exposing yourself to hormone-disrupting chemicals:"

**Narrative + Stat:**
"Burberry burned $36,500,000 of clothes in one year. Not because they couldn't sell them."

**Reversal + Authority:**
"Everyone worships Huberman. But Stanford's most brilliant professor isn't a neuroscientist."

## Hook Quality Checklist

Before committing to your opener, check:

1. **Scroll-stop test** — Would YOU stop for this? Be honest.
2. **Specificity** — Does it use a real number, name, or concrete detail? Vague hooks die.
3. **Curiosity gap** — Does the reader NEED to know what comes next? Or could they guess?
4. **Bold but defensible** — Can the body of the tweet actually back this claim?
5. **No throat-clearing** — Is the very first word doing work? Or is it setup?
6. **First sentence = strongest sentence** — If the rest of the tweet disappeared, would the first line still be interesting?

---

## Anti-Patterns (Hook Killers)

Never open a long-form tweet with:
- "Let me tell you about..."
- "I want to share..."
- "I've been thinking about..."
- "In today's world..."
- "Here's the thing about..."
- "Interesting thread on..."
- Any form of throat-clearing or apology

These are throat-clearing. They're the written equivalent of "um." Cut them and start with the actual thought.
