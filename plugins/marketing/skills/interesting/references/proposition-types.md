# The 12 Proposition Types (Index of the Interesting)

Each type works in BOTH directions (a→b or b→a). Choose based on what your audience currently assumes.

---

## 1. Organization

**Direction A:** What seems disorganized or chaotic is actually organized and structured
**Direction B:** What seems organized or structured is actually disorganized and chaotic

**Classic references:**
- Tönnies: Society (seemingly chaotic) is organized around Gemeinschaft and Gesellschaft
- Marx: Bourgeois society (seemingly organized) is actually chaotic class struggle

**Modern applications:**
- "Twitter discourse looks like chaos, but follows predictable outrage cycles"
- "Your 'organized' morning routine is actually random habit stacking"
- "The creator economy seems anarchic but has clear power laws"

---

## 2. Composition

**Direction A:** What seems like many different things is actually one underlying thing
**Direction B:** What seems like one thing is actually many different things

**Classic references:**
- Freud: Dreams, jokes, and slips (seemingly unrelated) all stem from instinctual drives
- Weber: Stratification (seemingly monolithic) is actually class + status + power combined

**Modern applications:**
- "Every viral tweet follows the same formula" (many → one)
- "Your 'one' target audience is actually 5 distinct buyer personas" (one → many)
- "All productivity systems are energy management in disguise"

---

## 3. Abstraction

**Direction A:** What seems individual or psychological is actually holistic or systemic
**Direction B:** What seems systemic or sociological is actually individual and psychological

**Classic references:**
- Durkheim: Suicide (seemingly individual choice) is a social phenomenon
- Freud: War (seemingly social) stems from individual psychological drives

**Modern applications:**
- "Your impostor syndrome isn't personal — it's structural" (individual → systemic)
- "Company culture problems are actually founder psychology problems" (systemic → individual)
- "Burnout isn't about workload, it's about identity"

---

## 4. Generalization

**Direction A:** What seems local or specific is actually universal
**Direction B:** What seems universal or general is actually local and specific

**Classic references:**
- Mannheim: Bourgeois ideology (seemingly universal) affects only one class
- Malinowski: Oedipus complex (seemingly universal) doesn't exist in all societies

**Modern applications:**
- "Best practices from FAANG don't apply to 99% of companies" (universal → local)
- "This 'niche' problem affects every knowledge worker" (local → universal)
- "Silicon Valley advice only works in Silicon Valley"

---

## 5. Stabilization

**Direction A:** What seems stable and permanent is actually unstable and changing
**Direction B:** What seems unstable and changing is actually stable and permanent

**Classic references:**
- Marx: Bourgeois society (seemingly permanent) is about to transform
- Simmel: Conflict-ridden organizations (seemingly unstable) maintain steady-state equilibrium

**Modern applications:**
- "The 'AI revolution' is following the same hype cycle as every tech before it" (changing → stable)
- "Your 'stable' career path is being disrupted right now" (stable → changing)
- "Remote work isn't a trend — it's a permanent restructuring"

---

## 6. Function

**Direction A:** What seems dysfunctional or ineffective actually serves a function and works
**Direction B:** What seems functional or effective is actually dysfunctional and counterproductive

**Classic references:**
- Merton: Political machines (seemingly corrupt) actually serve important community functions
- Marcuse: Tolerance (seemingly liberating) actually represses dissent

**Modern applications:**
- "Meetings work — you're just running them wrong" (broken → actually works)
- "That productivity app is making you LESS productive" (works → actually broken)
- "Procrastination is your brain protecting you from bad ideas"

---

## 7. Evaluation

**Direction A:** What seems bad is actually good
**Direction B:** What seems good is actually bad

**Classic references:**
- Laing: Schizophrenia (seemingly pathological) is a sane response to insane conditions
- Nietzsche: Christian morality (seemingly virtuous) is actually life-denying

**Modern applications:**
- "Your biggest weakness is your greatest competitive advantage" (bad → good)
- "That 'good habit' is destroying your creativity" (good → bad)
- "Failure is the only reliable teacher"

---

## 8. Correlation

**Direction A:** What seems unrelated is actually connected
**Direction B:** What seems related is actually unconnected

**Classic references:**
- Hollingshead: Social class and mental illness (seemingly unrelated) are correlated
- Durkheim: Suicide and weather or heredity (seemingly related) are actually uncorrelated

**Modern applications:**
- "Sleep quality predicts startup success better than IQ" (unrelated → connected)
- "Follower count has no correlation with revenue" (related → unconnected)
- "Your childhood hobby predicts your ideal career"

---

## 9. Co-existence

**Direction A:** What seems compatible can't actually exist together
**Direction B:** What seems incompatible can actually coexist

**Classic references:**
- de Rougemont: Love and marriage (seemingly compatible) are fundamentally incompatible
- Freud: Love and hate (seemingly incompatible) coexist as ambivalence

**Modern applications:**
- "You can't have work-life balance AND outlier success" (compatible → can't coexist)
- "High performance and sustainable pace can coexist" (incompatible → can coexist)
- "Growth and profitability are the same thing, not tradeoffs"

---

## 10. Co-variation

**Direction A:** What seems to increase together actually moves in opposite directions
**Direction B:** What seems to move in opposite directions actually increases together

**Classic references:**
- Caplovitz: As income decreases, expenditures actually increase for the poor
- Tocqueville: As living standards rise, revolutionary desire increases rather than decreasing

**Modern applications:**
- "The more you optimize, the less you achieve" (expected positive → actually negative)
- "The less you post, the more engagement you get" (expected negative → actually positive)
- "Working fewer hours increases output"

---

## 11. Opposition

**Direction A:** What seems similar is actually opposite
**Direction B:** What seems opposite is actually similar

**Classic references:**
- McLuhan: Hot and cool media (radio vs TV) are opposites though both are "media"
- Hoffer: True believers in opposing movements have identical psychological profiles

**Modern applications:**
- "Building in public and stealth mode achieve the same thing" (opposite → similar)
- "Your competitor is actually doing the opposite of what you think" (similar → opposite)
- "Hustle culture and anti-hustle culture come from the same place"

---

## 12. Causation

**Direction A:** What seems to be the cause is actually the effect
**Direction B:** What seems to be the effect is actually the cause

**Classic references:**
- Becker: Deviant behavior doesn't cause labeling — labeling causes deviant behavior
- Weber: Religion doesn't reflect economy — religion shapes economy (Protestant Ethic)

**Modern applications:**
- "Success doesn't create confidence — confidence creates success" (reverse causation)
- "You don't write because you have ideas — you have ideas because you write"
- "Culture doesn't follow strategy — strategy follows culture"

---

## Combining Types for Maximum Impact

The most compelling propositions stack two or more types:

| Combination | Example |
|-------------|---------|
| Evaluation + Causation | "That 'bad' habit isn't hurting your productivity — your productivity obsession is creating the habit" |
| Opposition + Co-variation | "As companies become more 'different,' they become more similar — and vice versa" |
| Abstraction + Generalization | "Your personal burnout is a universal systemic problem" |
| Function + Evaluation | "The thing you think is broken is the thing that's actually working — and the thing you trust is what's failing you" |
| Stabilization + Causation | "The 'disruption' didn't cause the change — the change was already happening and the 'disruption' just named it" |

---

## Testing Your Proposition

Run every proposition through these four checks:

1. **Can you name the specific assumption your audience holds?** If you can't articulate what they currently believe, you can't effectively deny it.
2. **Does your proposition specifically deny THAT assumption?** Not a different assumption, not a related assumption — that exact one.
3. **Is the denial calibrated correctly?** Too weak = obvious. Too strong = absurd. Too irrelevant = so what?
4. **Does it work on both levels?** Theoretical insight (changes what they think) AND practical implication (changes what they do).
