# 14 Delivery Techniques for Interesting Ideas

The angle is WHAT you say. The technique is HOW you say it. These 14 approaches determine whether your interesting idea actually lands or gets scrolled past.

---

## The Content Hierarchy

Before picking a technique, know what level you're aiming for:

| Level | Info | Presentation | Audience reaction |
|-------|------|-------------|-------------------|
| 1 (skip) | Obvious | Obvious | Eye roll |
| 2 (decent) | Obvious | Non-obvious | "Huh, never heard it put that way" |
| 3 (strong) | Non-obvious | Obvious | "Whoa, I didn't know that" |
| 4 (elite) | Non-obvious | Non-obvious | "This changed how I think" |

Level 2 = new wrapper on old info. Level 3 = surprising info, delivered straight. Level 4 = surprising info delivered in a surprising way. Always aim for 3 or 4.

---

## Technique 1: Powerful Analogy

Connect your idea to something from a completely different domain.

**How it works:** The unexpected comparison creates a bridge between something the audience understands and something they don't. The familiar illuminates the unfamiliar.

**Example:** "Growing your audience is like cultivating bamboo — 5 years of invisible nurturing, then 3 feet of growth in a day"

**Another example:** "Building a startup is like getting ripped — everyone wants the six pack but nobody wants to meal prep for 2 years straight"

**When to use:** When the core idea is abstract and needs grounding. When you want people to see something they already know in a completely new light.

**Watch out for:** Analogies that are too neat. Real insight is messy. If the analogy maps perfectly in every dimension, it's probably too cute.

---

## Technique 2: "Most People Think X, But Actually Y"

Direct assumption-denial, stated plainly.

**How it works:** Names the common belief explicitly, then presents the counterpoint with evidence. The directness creates clarity. No tricks — just a clean reversal.

**Example:** "Most people think growth happens linearly. In reality, it's like water heating to boiling — long plateaus then rapid transformation"

**Another example:** "Most people think AI will replace developers. Actually it's just making the barrier to entry so low that everyone becomes a developer, which makes being a GOOD developer even more valuable"

**When to use:** When the assumption is universally held and you have strong evidence for the reversal. Works best when the "actually" part is genuinely surprising, not just a reframe.

**Watch out for:** Weak "Actually Y" that's just a slight variation of X. The reversal has to be meaningful.

---

## Technique 3: Personification

Attach your insight to a memorable character, archetype, or real person.

**How it works:** Abstract advice becomes concrete when embodied by a recognizable figure. People remember characters better than concepts.

**Example:** "Your business needs a Simon Cowell — brutally honest, cutting through pleasantries"

**Another example:** "Your product needs a Gordon Ramsay in the testing phase — someone who will call your UI 'fucking raw' before you ship it to users"

**When to use:** When the insight is about a quality or approach that's easier to show through a person than to describe in the abstract.

**Watch out for:** Obscure references your audience won't know. The character has to be instantly recognizable.

---

## Technique 4: Story-First

Lead with a specific moment, scene, or anecdote before revealing the lesson.

**How it works:** Stories bypass the audience's critical filter. When someone hears "I spent 3 years building in public..." they're pulled into the narrative. The lesson arrives after they're already invested.

**Example:** "When I was 23, I watched fortunes get minted in the App Store gold rush..."

**Another example:** "I spent 3 years building in public and the one post that changed everything was the one I almost didn't publish because it felt too vulnerable"

**When to use:** When you have a real, specific experience that illustrates the insight. Works best when the story has tension — a moment where things could have gone either way.

**Watch out for:** Fictional or generic stories. "Imagine a founder named Sarah..." doesn't have the same pull as a real moment.

---

## Technique 5: Preframing

Borrow credibility from a respected source before delivering the insight.

**How it works:** The audience's openness to an idea increases when they believe it comes from a credible source. The frame sets up the insight to land harder.

**Example:** "Tony Robbins' top salesperson told me..." followed by the insight

**Another example:** "Talked to a founder making $500k/month who told me his secret is..."

**When to use:** When the insight alone might seem too simple or too bold. The credibility of the source gives the audience permission to take it seriously.

**Watch out for:** Name-dropping without substance. The preframe raises expectations — the insight has to deliver.

---

## Technique 6: Weird, Hyper-Specific Names

Create curiosity through unusual, highly specific naming.

**How it works:** A name like "The 30,000-Feet-Coke-Nerf Phenomenon" forces the audience to stop and ask "what the hell is that?" Curiosity is a more powerful hook than information.

**Example:** "The 30,000-Feet-Coke-Nerf Phenomenon"

**Another example:** "I call this the 'Excel Spreadsheet Stockholm Syndrome' — where knowledge workers defend the tools that waste 40% of their day"

**When to use:** When you want maximum curiosity pull. When the concept is something you've named yourself and want to make memorable and ownable.

**Watch out for:** Names that are weird without being descriptive. The name should make the audience curious AND give a hint of what it's about.

---

## Technique 7: Strategic Ridicule

Use absurd numbers, extreme specificity, or over-the-top claims to stop the scroll.

**How it works:** Numbers that seem impossibly precise or extreme create an involuntary "wait, really?" response. The ridiculousness is the hook.

**Example:** "72 hook frameworks" or "I spent $0.01 on ads"

**Another example:** "I've tested 847 different prompts and 99% of them are garbage. Here are the 3 that actually work"

**When to use:** When you want maximum pattern-interrupt. When you have real (even if cherry-picked) numbers that sound absurd but are true.

**Watch out for:** Exaggeration that's obviously fake. The number or claim has to be technically defensible, even if the framing is provocative.

---

## Technique 8: Outcome over Mechanism

Lead with what the audience gets, not how it works.

**How it works:** People care about results before they care about process. "How to make 50% more sales" pulls harder than "Write at 6th-grade level" even though the second explains the mechanism behind the first.

**Example:** "How to make 50% more sales" instead of "Write at 6th-grade level"

**Another example:** "How to 10x your content output without feeling like an AI slop factory"

**When to use:** For headlines, hooks, and opening lines where you need immediate pull. The mechanism can come after — once they're invested.

**Watch out for:** Promising outcomes you can't deliver. The outcome claim has to be real.

---

## Technique 9: Scientific Anchor

Open with an unknown research finding, then connect it to everyday experience.

**How it works:** Science carries authority. An unexpected research finding creates the "I didn't know that" reaction, and connecting it to their daily reality makes it feel personally relevant.

**Example:** "Scientists discovered that our brains have two distinct networks: the Default Mode Network..."

**Another example:** "Neuroscientists found that your brain literally can't distinguish between scrolling social media and gambling. Same dopamine pathways. Which explains why we're all fucked"

**When to use:** When there's genuine research that supports your point. When the audience respects evidence over opinion.

**Watch out for:** Misrepresenting studies. Pop science that oversimplifies. If the audience includes experts, they'll call out bad citations.

---

## Technique 10: Ancient Wisdom

Connect your insight to historical stories, concepts, or figures — especially from ancient civilizations.

**How it works:** Historical precedent gives ideas weight. If the Romans dealt with the same problem 2,000 years ago, it validates the insight as timeless rather than trendy.

**Example:** "When Julius Caesar crossed the Rubicon..."

**Another example:** "The Romans had a concept called 'otium' — purposeful leisure that recharges creativity. Meanwhile we call it 'being unproductive' and feel guilty. They built an empire. We doom-scroll."

**When to use:** When the insight is about human nature, decision-making, or organizational behavior — things that haven't fundamentally changed. Greek and Roman references work best because they carry cultural weight.

**Watch out for:** Historical comparisons that are superficial or inaccurate. The connection has to be genuine, not forced.

---

## Technique 11: Strategic Tension

Set up two opposing approaches, show both failing, then resolve with a third option.

**How it works:** Binary choices create natural tension. The audience picks a side, then realizes both are wrong. The resolution (a third path) feels like a discovery they made themselves.

**Example:** "Meet Sarah (quarterly focus) vs David (decade vision) → Neither works. The answer is Nested Time Horizons"

**Another example:** "Meet Founder A who ships daily and burns out in 6 months. Meet Founder B who plans for 6 months and never ships. The answer isn't balance, it's 'compressed sprints' — 2 weeks of insane shipping, 1 week of complete rest"

**When to use:** When the common debate presents two options and you have a third. When both popular approaches have visible downsides your audience has experienced.

**Watch out for:** False dichotomies. If one side of the tension is obviously straw-manned, the whole thing falls apart.

---

## Technique 12: Strategic Paradox

Present a genuine contradiction without forcing a clean resolution.

**How it works:** Paradoxes stick in people's minds because they can't be dismissed. There's no easy answer, which means the audience keeps thinking about it — and keeps sharing it to see what others think.

**Example:** "The Work-Life Balance Paradox: The more successful you become, the more you need rest but the less time you have"

**Another example:** "The content creator paradox: the more you optimize your content for algorithms, the less authentic it becomes. But the more authentic you are, the less the algorithm shows it. Nobody has solved this"

**When to use:** When you've identified a real tension that doesn't have a clean answer. When intellectual honesty is more valuable than a neat takeaway.

**Watch out for:** Fake paradoxes that are actually just tradeoffs. A real paradox has no clean resolution — both sides are simultaneously true and incompatible.

---

## Technique 13: Illuminating Question

Ask a question that makes the reader arrive at your conclusion themselves.

**How it works:** Self-generated conclusions are held more strongly than conclusions handed to you. The right question makes the insight feel like their discovery, not your lecture.

**Example:** "How do you expect employees to follow systems when you don't even know what those systems are?"

**Another example:** "How can you claim AI will replace developers when you can't even describe what you want the AI to build?"

**When to use:** When the conclusion is powerful but might create resistance if stated directly. When the audience is sophisticated and doesn't like being told what to think.

**Watch out for:** Questions that are obviously leading. The best illuminating questions feel like genuine inquiry, even though they point in a clear direction.

---

## Technique 14: "Screw the Obvious"

List all the obvious, tired advice on a topic — then dismantle it.

**How it works:** By acknowledging (and dismissing) what everyone else says, you position yourself as someone who's gone past the surface. The audience thinks "finally, someone who sees through the BS."

**Example:** "Every article tells you to 'add value first.' Here's why that advice is trash..."

**Another example:** "Everyone says 'just be consistent.' Cool. Consistent at what? Posting garbage daily? That's how you become forgettable. Here's what actually matters..."

**When to use:** When your topic has been beaten to death with cliche advice. When your audience is already jaded from hearing the same thing everywhere. When you have something genuinely different to offer after tearing down the obvious.

**Watch out for:** Tearing down advice without offering something better. If you just destroy the conventional wisdom and leave a vacuum, you've been provocative without being useful.

## Matching Technique to Situation

| Situation | Best techniques |
|-----------|----------------|
| LinkedIn post (professional audience) | #2 (Most People Think), #5 (Preframing), #11 (Strategic Tension) |
| Twitter/X (scroll-stopping needed) | #6 (Weird Names), #7 (Strategic Ridicule), #13 (Illuminating Question) |
| Newsletter hook | #4 (Story-First), #9 (Scientific Anchor), #10 (Ancient Wisdom) |
| Talk or presentation opener | #1 (Powerful Analogy), #12 (Strategic Paradox), #4 (Story-First) |
| Sales page headline | #8 (Outcome over Mechanism), #2 (Most People Think), #14 (Screw the Obvious) |
| Podcast episode hook | #4 (Story-First), #5 (Preframing), #9 (Scientific Anchor) |
| YouTube thumbnail/title | #7 (Strategic Ridicule), #6 (Weird Names), #8 (Outcome over Mechanism) |

## Stacking Techniques

The best content combines two techniques:

| Combo | Example |
|-------|---------|
| Story-First + Causation Flip | "I spent 2 years being 'consistent' and went nowhere. The month I stopped posting and started studying my audience, everything changed. Consistency doesn't create success — early wins create consistency." |
| Scientific Anchor + Powerful Analogy | "Neuroscientists found that willpower depletes like a battery. Your morning routine isn't discipline — it's spending your charge on easy things before the hard ones arrive." |
| Preframing + Screw the Obvious | "A founder doing $2M/year told me something that contradicts every piece of productivity advice you've ever read..." |
| Weird Name + Strategic Paradox | "I call it the 'Visibility Trap': the more visible you become, the less time you have to create the work that made you visible." |
