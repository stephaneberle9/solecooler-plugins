# Sequence Playbooks — Full Breakdown

Six sequence types with progression, schedule, framework, and anti-pattern for each. Pick one based on the user's situation in the **Pick** step of the main process.

---

## 1. Welcome Path (5-7 emails)

First impressions set the trajectory. This is the foundation everything else builds on. Use it the moment someone opts in for a freebie.

### What it accomplishes

Hands over the freebie, sets expectations for future emails, starts the relationship, identifies who's engaged, and quietly lays groundwork for the paid offer.

### The progression

Hand Off → Bond → Teach → Teach → Gap → Gentle Ask → Direct Ask

### Framework

| Email | Label | Job | Timing |
|-------|-------|-----|--------|
| 1 | Hand Off | Deliver the freebie + one quick action step | Immediately |
| 2 | Bond | Share a personal story that builds rapport | Day 2 |
| 3 | Teach | Give a useful lesson — create a quick win | Day 4 |
| 4 | Teach Again | Different topic, same value. Builds authority | Day 6 |
| 5 | Gap | Show what's possible beyond the freebie | Day 8 |
| 6 | Gentle Ask | Introduce the offer, let them self-select | Day 10 |
| 7 | Direct Ask | Make the clear pitch. One CTA. | Day 12 |

For email-by-email structures, subject line formulas, and section-by-section breakdowns, see [welcome-path.md](welcome-path.md).

### Anti-pattern

Pitching in email 1 because "they're warmest now." They opted in for the freebie, not your sales pitch. Lead with delivery and a quick win. The pitch comes after trust has been earned over emails 2-5.

Other failure modes:
- Sending the offer in email 2 or 3 — too early, breaks trust
- Skipping the Bond email and going straight to teaching — never feels like a person
- Asking for a reply in every email — dilutes the one micro-engagement that matters in email 1

## 2. Sales Run (4-7 emails)

Use when the relationship is warm — either right after the welcome path or as its own campaign for an existing list.

### The progression

Announce → Want → Evidence → Doubt → Deadline → Final Push → Closing Bell

### Framework

| Email | Label | Job |
|-------|-------|-----|
| 1 | Announce | Present the offer and its core promise |
| 2 | Want | Paint the transformation, show the gap between now and after |
| 3 | Evidence | Testimonials, results, case studies |
| 4 | Doubt | Handle the biggest objection head-on |
| 5 | Deadline | Explain why acting now matters (only if real) |
| 6 | Final Push | Last CTA, clear and direct |
| 7 | Closing Bell | Deadline reminder — only if there's an actual deadline |

**Pacing:** Every 2 days normally. Daily during launches. Final 3 emails in 3 days if there's a deadline.

### Anti-pattern

Manufacturing fake urgency ("this week only!" when nothing actually changes next week). Readers can smell it, and once they catch you lying about urgency they distrust everything else. Either have a real deadline or skip the Deadline email entirely. The Final Push works without urgency.

Other failure modes:
- Using "Doubt" email to defend yourself instead of addressing the buyer's real fear
- Stacking 5 testimonials in one email instead of one strong story per email
- Same subject line pattern across all 7 — inbox blindness sets in by email 3

## 3. Campaign Blitz (6-10 emails)

For launches, cohort opens, promotions — anything with a hard close date.

### The progression

Tease → Announce → Prove → Handle → Close

### Framework

**Pre-launch (1-2 emails):** Build anticipation. Hint that something's coming without revealing details.

**Doors open (2-3 emails):** Full announcement with details. Deep-dive on the transformation. Social proof and testimonials.

**Mid-campaign (2-3 emails):** Objection handling. A case study or story. "Is this right for me?" email.

**Doors close (2-3 emails):** 48-hour warning. Final testimonial. Last call on deadline day.

### Campaign timeline

| Day | Email | Notes |
|-----|-------|-------|
| -3 | Tease | Optional. Build curiosity |
| -1 | Tomorrow | "Something's coming" |
| 0 (AM) | Doors open | Full announcement |
| 0 (PM) | Different angle | Same offer, different framing |
| 2 | Deep-dive | Transformation and value |
| 4 | Proof | Social proof and results |
| 5 | Objections | Address the "but..." |
| 6 | 48 hours | Urgency kicks in |
| 7 (AM) | 24 hours | Morning reminder |
| 7 (PM) | Final hours | Evening push |
| 7 (late) | Last call | Midnight deadline |

### Anti-pattern

Going dark for the close. Most revenue comes in the final 48 hours. People who flinched on day 0 buy on day 7. Cutting the closing emails because "I don't want to annoy people" cuts the launch in half. The unsubscribers were never going to buy anyway.

Other failure modes:
- Tease emails that reveal the offer (defeats the purpose)
- Skipping the "different angle" email on day 0 PM — costs you the segment that didn't connect with the morning frame
- One email on close day instead of three — leaves money on the table

## 4. Win-Back Series (3-4 emails)

For people who stopped opening. 30+ days of silence.

### The progression

Surprise → Give → Ask → Goodbye

### Framework

| Email | Label | Job |
|-------|-------|-----|
| 1 | Surprise | Break the pattern. Acknowledge the silence. Different subject line style than usual |
| 2 | Give | Best content you've got. Pure value. No ask |
| 3 | Ask | Direct question: do you still want these emails? |
| 4 | Goodbye | Removing them from the list. Creates real urgency |

**Subject line ideas for win-backs:**
- "Did I lose you?"
- "Want me to stop emailing?"
- "This is awkward"
- "You're about to miss [specific thing]"
- "[First name], still around?"

**Pacing:** Days 0, 3, 7, 10. Space to respond between each.

### Anti-pattern

Refusing to send the Goodbye email because "what if they come back later?" They won't. A bloated list of dead subscribers tanks deliverability for the people who DO read you. The Goodbye email also re-engages more readers than any other email in the series — loss aversion is real. Send it.

Other failure modes:
- Using the same subject-line style as your normal emails (the whole point is pattern interrupt)
- Pitching anything in this sequence except in the Ask email
- Running win-back monthly — once per quarter is the right cadence

## 5. Trust Builder (4-6 emails)

Pure value emails between welcome and any sales push. Use when the welcome path ended without a buy and you need to deepen the relationship before pitching again.

### The progression

Story → Lesson → Case → Framework → Question → Transition

### Framework

| Email | Label | Job |
|-------|-------|-----|
| 1 | Story | Personal story that reveals how you think. No CTA, no ask. |
| 2 | Lesson | One useful lesson the reader can apply this week. Plant the "I could actually do this" feeling. |
| 3 | Case | A real example — yours or a customer's — with specific numbers. |
| 4 | Framework | A repeatable structure they can steal. Most shareable email in the sequence. |
| 5 | Question | Ask for a reply. "What are you stuck on right now?" — feeds future content and reactivates. |
| 6 | Transition | Connect the dots between your lessons and where you help. Soft. Sets up the next sales run. |

**Pacing:** 1-2 emails per week. Steady rhythm. No urgency markers.

**Rule:** Zero direct asks until email 6. This sequence earns, it doesn't sell.

### Anti-pattern

Sneaking pitches into the value emails ("by the way, my course covers this in depth"). Once you do this once, the reader treats every Trust Builder email as a setup. The whole point of this sequence is to re-establish that you give without asking. Pitch in email 6 — that's the contract.

Other failure modes:
- Generic teaching that could come from anyone — Trust Builder must reveal HOW you specifically think
- No specific numbers or named situations in the Case email — it reads like a hypothetical
- The Question email asking something vague ("how can I help?") instead of specific ("what are you stuck on this week?")

## 6. Buyer Onboarding (4-6 emails)

After purchase. Reduces refunds, creates successful users, sets up upsells.

### The progression

Welcome → Quick Win → Deep Use → Check-In → Testimonial Ask → Upsell

### Framework

| Email | Label | Job | Timing |
|-------|-------|-----|--------|
| 1 | Welcome | Confirm purchase, set expectations, give one immediate action | Immediately |
| 2 | Quick Win | The easiest thing to do in the product that produces a visible result | Day 1 |
| 3 | Deep Use | The one feature most buyers miss. Drives activation. | Day 3 |
| 4 | Check-In | "How's it going?" — real reply invitation. Catches refund risks before they happen. | Day 7 |
| 5 | Testimonial Ask | If they've hit their first win, ask for the testimonial while the emotion is fresh | Day 14 |
| 6 | Upsell | Next-step offer that makes sense for users who got value | Day 21+ |

**Rule:** Track email 4 replies carefully. Every reply mentioning a problem is a refund warning — reach out personally.

**Skip the upsell email** if the buyer shows low engagement in emails 1-4. Pitching an upsell to a struggling buyer creates churn, not revenue.

### Anti-pattern

Skipping the Check-In because "I don't want to remind unhappy customers they could refund." Backwards. The customers who would refund are already drafting the email. The Check-In gives you a chance to fix the problem before they hit "request refund." This email saves more revenue than the Upsell makes.

Other failure modes:
- Quick Win email that requires 30 minutes of setup — defeats the point
- Asking for a testimonial on Day 1 before they've experienced value
- Upselling at Day 3 — too soon, kills the relationship
