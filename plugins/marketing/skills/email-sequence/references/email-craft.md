# Email Craft — Subject Lines, Copy Rules, and Open-Rate Tactics

---

## subject line playbook

### Curiosity gap
Opens a question the reader can't answer without opening.
- "The [X] mistake that cost me [Y]"
- "Why [surprising thing] actually works"
- "I was wrong about [topic]"

### Straight benefit
Tells them exactly what they'll get.
- "How to [outcome] in [timeframe]"
- "[Number] ways to [benefit]"
- "The fastest path to [result]"

### Story tease
Makes them curious about what happened.
- "Quick story about [topic]"
- "What happened when I [action]"
- "The email I almost didn't write"

### Direct question
Feels personal. Hard to ignore.
- "Can I ask you something?"
- "What would you do with [outcome]?"
- "Are you making this mistake?"

### Real urgency
Only works when the deadline is actual.
- "[X] hours left"
- "Closing tonight"
- "Last chance: [offer]"

### Pattern interrupt
Breaks the visual pattern of their inbox.
- "." (just a period)
- "So..."
- "Bad news"
- "[First name]"

---

## what tanks your open rate

- WRITING IN ALL CAPS
- Overusing punctuation!!!
- "Newsletter #47" or any numbered edition format
- "[BRAND NAME] Weekly Update"
- Clickbait that doesn't deliver inside the email
- Using the same subject line format every single time

## copy principles

### preview text strategy
The first 40-90 characters after the subject line show in most email clients. This is your second headline. Use it to continue the curiosity from the subject line.

Bad preview: "Having trouble viewing this email?"
Good preview: Extends the subject line's hook or adds a new one.

### open loops
Plant curiosity that pulls them into the next email.
- "I'll show you why tomorrow."
- "That's not even the interesting part."
- "The third one caught me off guard."

### specificity principle
Specific numbers and details make claims feel real. Vague claims feel like marketing.

| Vague (weak) | Specific (strong) |
|-------------|-------------------|
| Made money | $47,329 in one launch |
| Many customers | 2,847 customers |
| Recently | Last Tuesday |
| A lot of growth | 340% increase in 6 weeks |

### paragraph length
1-3 sentences maximum per paragraph. People scan emails on their phone. Long paragraphs get skipped entirely.

### the P.S. advantage
Close to half of email readers look at the P.S. before they read the body. Treat it as prime real estate.

Good uses for a P.S.:
- Restate the main CTA
- Add a second hook or angle
- Drop a personal note
- Remind them of a deadline

### single CTA rule
Every email should drive exactly one action. When you give people two links to click or two things to do, they often do neither.

The one exception: a delivery email can include both "download your thing" and "reply and tell me about yourself."
