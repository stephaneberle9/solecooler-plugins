# Comment Craft — how reach actually works on LinkedIn comments

A comment is not a small post. It behaves differently in the feed, the algorithm reads it differently, and length works against you in ways it doesn't in your own posts. This file explains the mechanics so every comment is built to be seen.

---

## 1. A comment does two jobs — know which one you're optimizing for

When you comment on someone else's post, the comment first counts as an **engagement signal on their post**. That's its primary effect: it pushes the *original* post's visibility up, and it puts *you* in front of that post's audience. This is why commenting on the right posts (large or fast-growing accounts in your space) is a visibility play in itself — even before your comment earns anything on its own.

For your comment to pull reach *of its own*, it has to clear two separate hurdles. Most comments fail at least one:

### Hurdle 1 — it has to get read

LinkedIn truncates comments after just a few lines with a "**…more**" link. Most people never click it. So:

- A long block gets **scrolled past**. The reader sees a wall, decides it's not worth the tap, moves on.
- A short comment gets **read in full**, because all of it is above the fold.

The practical consequence: **the first one to two lines must deliver the entire value on their own**, with no "…more" click required. If your point only lands after the cutoff, for most readers it never lands at all.

### Hurdle 2 — it has to earn its own reactions

The algorithm ranks comments. A comment that collects likes and replies gets tagged a **"Top comment"** and is shown **higher on the post and to more people**. A comment that earns nothing sinks below the fold and dies regardless of how good it was.

What earns reactions: **a pointed, self-contained statement carrying a concrete fact or a clear thesis.** Something a reader can react to. A mini-essay gives the reader nothing to grab — it's too much to like and too vague to reply to. One sharp claim or one new fact outperforms five hedged paragraphs.

---

## 2. The rule of thumb

> The first 1–2 lines must work **without** the "…more" click and must deliver real value on their own — a new fact, a concrete number, a sharp counter-point, or a question worth answering.

Everything after the cutoff is a bonus for the minority who expand. Never put the payload there.

---

## 3. Length

Shorter than feels natural. Treat the visible-before-cutoff zone as the whole comment, and anything below it as optional supporting detail.

- **1–3 short lines** is the sweet spot for a comment meant to earn reactions.
- If you genuinely need more (a worked example, a list, a source), put the **headline value first**, then let the rest run below the fold for the readers who expand.
- Never bury the point in the middle of a paragraph. Front, always.

---

## 4. Links

External links tend to **dampen reach** — LinkedIn would rather keep people on-platform. The effect is real but **much weaker in a comment than in your own post**, so a link in a comment is far less costly than a link in a post.

Still, play it safe:

- Put the link **at the end**, after the value has already landed.
- Make the comment work even if the link is never clicked. The first lines should stand alone.
- One link max. A comment stuffed with links reads like spam and gets treated like it.

---

## 5. What earns "Top comment" — archetypes that work

Each of these gives the reader something concrete to react to. See [templates/comment-archetypes.md](../templates/comment-archetypes.md) for fill-in structures.

- **The new fact** — add a concrete data point, source, or detail the post didn't have. ("There's actually a 2024 ruling on exactly this — …")
- **The sharp counter** — a respectful, specific disagreement or nuance. Not "I disagree" but *why*, in one line.
- **The build-on** — extend the author's point one step further than they took it. Makes you look like you got there first.
- **The lived experience** — a short, concrete first-hand result or war story. Specific beats general.
- **The reframe** — restate the post's idea in a sharper, more memorable way the author will want to pin.
- **The precise question** — a question the author (and other readers) actually want to answer. Drives replies, which the algorithm loves.

---

## 6. What kills a comment

- **Generic praise** — "Great post!", "So true", "Thanks for sharing", "Love this 🙌". Invisible. Earns nothing, ranks nowhere. If that's all you have, don't comment.
- **The mini-essay** — too long to be read, too diffuse to react to. Fails both hurdles at once.
- **Pitching** — turning someone else's post into an ad for your thing. Readers and the author both punish it. Add value; the visibility is the reward.
- **Restating the post** — agreeing by repeating what they already said adds nothing to react to.
- **Burying the lede** — the one good line sitting in sentence four, below the cutoff.

---

## 7. Be a good guest

You're commenting on someone else's post. Match their register — don't hijack the thread, don't make it about you, don't out-shout the author. The best comments make the *author* look good for having sparked them, which is exactly why authors reply to them (another reach signal).

- Tagging the author (`@Name`) can lift visibility when it's natural — not as a gimmick.
- Commenting **early** on a post that's gaining traction gives your comment more time to accumulate reactions before the post peaks. First substantive comment often wins the thread.

---

## 8. Voice still matters

A comment is short, so voice shows up fast — one off-key word and it reads like a bot. It still has to sound like the user wrote it: their phrasing, their level of directness, no corporate filler, no AI tells. Pull from the user's voice context exactly as the post skill does.
