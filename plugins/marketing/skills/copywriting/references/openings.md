# Opening Lines

The first sentence has one job: get them to read the second sentence.

---

## The Direct Challenge

Open by challenging what they believe:

> "You've been using Claude wrong."
> "Everything you know about pricing is backwards."
> "Most marketing advice is designed to make you feel busy, not successful."

**Why:** Stops the scroll. Creates tension. Self-selects readers who suspect you might be right.

**Formula:**
- "You've been [doing X] wrong."
- "Most [people/companies] [get this wrong]."
- "[Common belief] is actually [wrong/backwards/dangerous]."

---

## The Story Opening

> "Last Tuesday, I opened my laptop and saw a number I couldn't believe: $47,329 in one day."
> "It was 2 AM and I was still coding. The launch was in 6 hours. I had no idea if anyone would show up."
> "I was $78,000 in debt, working 70-hour weeks, and my marriage was falling apart."

**Why:** Reader is IN the scene before they know they're reading sales copy.

**Formula:** [Specific moment] + [Tension or curiosity] + [Implied transformation]

---

## The Confession

Vulnerability disarms skepticism:

> "I'll be honest with you. I almost gave up on this business three times."
> "Here's something embarrassing: I used to think copywriting was about being clever."
> "I made every mistake in the book. Spent $50,000 on ads that didn't work."

**Why:** Honesty creates trust. Reader thinks "they're like me." Sets up the transformation.

---

## The Specific Result

Lead with proof:

> "Last month, this landing page generated $127,394 in sales."
> "In 9 months, we did $400k+ on a vibe-coded website using these exact methods."

**Formula:** [Specific result] + [Implied "here's how"]

---

## The Question Opening

Enter the conversation in their head:

> "Have you ever stared at a blank page, knowing you need to write something that sells... and just froze?"
> "What would you do with an extra 10 hours a week?"

**Why:** If the question matches their reality, they're hooked. Creates internal "yes." Self-selects the right reader.

**Formula:**
- "Have you ever [common struggle]?"
- "Do you ever wonder why [comparison/frustration]?"

---

## The Contrarian Opening

Flip conventional wisdom:

> "Stop writing headlines."
> "The best marketing advice is to do less marketing."

**Why:** Pattern interrupt. Creates "wait, what?" response.

**Warning:** Must pay off. Contrarian opening + conventional content = disappointment.

---

## The "Imagine" Opening

Future-pace into the transformation:

> "Imagine waking up tomorrow and your landing page has already made 3 sales while you slept."
> "Picture this: you open your inbox and see 47 new leads from one email."

**Why:** Reader experiences the benefit emotionally before the pitch.

---

## The Pattern Interrupt

Break expectations:

> "Don't read this."
> "This isn't for you."
> "I'm going to try to talk you OUT of buying this."

**Why:** Reverse psychology. Breaks the "sales pitch" pattern.

---

## The Time-Stamp Opening

Creates immediacy:

> "It's 4:37 AM and I can't sleep. I keep thinking about what I'm about to share with you."
> "I'm writing this from gate B7 at LAX, 20 minutes before my flight boards."

**Why:** Feels personal, not scripted. Reader feels they're getting something fresh.

---

## The Short-Sentence Opening

Sugarman's approach — first sentence should be stupidly easy to read:

> "It's simple."
> "Here's the truth."
> "This works."
> "Let me explain."

**Why:** No friction to start reading. Reader is into paragraph two before they realize it.

---

## The "Math" Opening

Quantify the problem:

> "5 minutes explaining your business + 10 minutes prompting + 15 minutes editing = hours of your week, every week, forever."
> "You're losing $1,847 every month. Here's the math."

**Formula:** "[Problem math] = [Painful total]"

---

## Openings to Avoid

These scream "AI wrote this":

- "In today's fast-paced world..."
- "Are you ready to take your business to the next level?"
- "Welcome! I'm so glad you're here."
- "Have you ever wanted to..."
- "What if I told you..."
- "The secret they don't want you to know..."
- "In this article, you'll learn..."
- "Let's dive in!"

These are generic. They could be about anything. They don't demonstrate understanding of the reader.

---

## The Meta-Principle

A strong opening does at least one of these:

1. **Matches their mental state** (enters the conversation in their head)
2. **Creates curiosity** (opens a loop they need to close)
3. **Makes a bold claim** (backed up later with proof)
4. **Tells a story** (they see themselves in)
5. **Challenges a belief** (they're not sure about anyway)

If your opening does none of these, rewrite it.
