# Topic Mining Playbook

Detailed techniques for growing your keyword list and stress-testing your topic anchors.

---

## the prism scan (6 angles)

Every starter keyword gets examined through six different angles. Each one pulls out terms you'd never find thinking only about your product.

**Angle 1: what you sell**
The straightforward stuff. Your courses, programs, services.
> "8-week cooking bootcamp", "virtual chef lessons", "meal prep course"

**Angle 2: what frustrates them**
The problems your audience punches into Google at 11 PM.
> "everything I cook tastes bland", "can't follow recipes", "wasting money on DoorDash", "kitchen anxiety"

**Angle 3: what they're chasing**
The end state, not the journey. The transformation photo.
> "cook restaurant meals at home", "healthy dinners in 20 minutes", "confidently host a dinner party"

**Angle 4: what separates you**
Your specific angle. The reason someone picks you over 10,000 alternatives.
> "cooking for total beginners", "only 30-minute meals", "chef who couldn't boil water at 25"

**Angle 5: what else they explore**
Topics your audience cares about beyond your core offering.
> "kitchen organization", "grocery budgeting", "wine pairing basics", "food photography tips"

**Angle 6: names worth linking to**
People, brands, tools, and methods you want associated with your content.
> "Gordon Ramsay techniques", "Blue Apron alternative", "MasterClass cooking review", "cast iron cooking"

## multiplication patterns

After the prism scan, run each keyword through these formats to generate variations:

### question formats

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| what is [keyword] | what is meal prepping |
| how to [keyword] | how to cook steak perfectly |
| why [keyword] | why does my food taste bland |
| best [keyword] | best cooking classes online |
| [keyword] vs [alternative] | meal prep vs cooking fresh |
| [keyword] examples | quick dinner examples |
| [keyword] for [audience] | cooking for college students |

### modifier formats

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| [keyword] tools | meal prep tools |
| [keyword] templates | weekly meal plan templates |
| [keyword] guide | beginner cooking guide |
| [keyword] strategy | meal planning strategy |
| [keyword] [current year] | best cooking courses 2026 |
| [keyword] for beginners | knife skills for beginners |
| [keyword] for [niche] | cooking for one person |

### comparison formats

| Pattern | Example |
|---------|---------|
| [keyword A] vs [keyword B] | online classes vs in-person lessons |
| best [category] | best online cooking schools |
| [brand] alternatives | Blue Apron alternatives |
| [brand] review | MasterClass cooking review |

Aim to expand from your initial starters to 100-200 total keywords.

## anchor validation

Before committing to an anchor, run it through four gates. Most people skip this and end up producing content that nobody ever searches for.

### gate 1: do people actually search this?

Does the anchor pull in meaningful volume across its keyword cluster?

| Result | Action |
|--------|--------|
| Yes, meaningful demand exists | Valid anchor, proceed |
| No, minimal or no searches | Downgrade to single article, or cut entirely |

Classic trap: choosing your proprietary method name as an anchor because you're attached to it. Nobody types your method name into Google. They type the problem it solves.

### gate 2: their words or your words?

| What you call it (wrong) | What they search (right) |
|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| "Our signature cooking system" | "learn to cook at home" |
| "[Brand name] recipes" | "easy dinner recipes" |
| "Why our method is different" | "best way to learn cooking" |
| Feature descriptions | Outcome-focused searches |

Write content using the language your market actually types into search bars.

### gate 3: realistic competition check

Open the top three results for your anchor keyword:

| What you see | What it means |
|-------------|---------------|
| All heavyweight sites (Food Network, NYT Cooking, Bon Appetit) | Find a tighter angle or different anchor |
| Mix of established and smaller publishers | Winnable if your content is genuinely better |
| Thin content from unknown sources | Wide open, move fast |

### gate 4: what's your edge?

| Your advantage | Priority level |
|---------------|---------------|
| Original data or documented student results | Prioritize heavily |
| A teaching method you developed yourself | Prioritize heavily |
| Practitioner experience (you actually cook daily, not just theorize) | Prioritize |
| Recycled information available everywhere | Deprioritize |

If you've taught 200 students to cook and have their before/after stories, "cooking for beginners" is your anchor. If you're summarizing recipes from AllRecipes, you're competing on even ground with everyone.

### documenting validation

For each proposed anchor:

- **Anchor:** [Name]
- **Search demand:** PASS/FAIL
- **Audience language:** PASS/FAIL
- **Competitive reality:** PASS/FAIL
- **Unfair advantage:** YES/NO

**Two or more failures disqualifies it as an anchor.** Either fold the keywords into a single supporting article, or drop them.

## free tools for checking your work

All of these cost nothing:

| Tool | What it tells you |
|------|-------------------|
| **Google Trends** | Whether interest is growing or declining |
| **Google Search** (autocomplete + "People Also Ask") | Free keyword suggestions straight from the source |
| **AnswerThePublic** (free tier) | Question-based keyword ideas mapped visually |
| **AlsoAsked** (free tier) | How questions branch and relate to each other |
| **Reddit and Quora** | The real language people use when discussing your topic |
